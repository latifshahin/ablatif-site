How Banks Evaluate a Loan Case in Bangladesh: Eligibility, Documentation, and Common Borrower Mistakes

In Bangladesh, banks follow a structured and risk-focused process when evaluating any loan proposal, whether it is for personal, SME, or corporate lending. Understanding this process helps borrowers prepare better and reduces unnecessary delays or rejections.

1. Eligibility and Basic Assessment
Banks first verify the borrower’s identity, profession, age, and banking history. For salaried individuals, job stability, length of service, and employer reputation matter. For business clients, trade license validity, business age, and transaction behavior are closely reviewed. A clean CIB (Credit Information Bureau) report is mandatory.

2. Required Documentation
Common documents include NID, photographs, bank statements (usually last 6–12 months), salary certificate or pay slips, TIN certificate, and utility bills. For business loans, banks require trade license, VAT registration (if applicable), financial statements, and account turnover evidence.

3. Income Verification and EMI Capacity
Banks do not consider gross income alone. Net disposable income after regular expenses and existing loan installments is crucial. Generally, banks prefer total monthly EMI to remain within a safe range (often 30–40% of verified net income).

Example:
If a borrower earns BDT 60,000 per month and already pays BDT 12,000 as EMI, the bank may allow a new EMI of only BDT 6,000–10,000, even if the salary seems sufficient. This ensures financial stability and reduces default risk.

4. Risk Signals Banks Watch Carefully
Irregular account transactions, frequent cash withdrawals, unpaid utility bills, over-dependence on loans, and multiple recent credit inquiries raise red flags. These factors can lead to reduced loan limits or rejection.

5. Why Banks Sometimes Reduce Loan Limits
Even when income appears adequate, banks may lower approved limits due to unstable income sources, short job tenure, weak business cash flow, or high household expenses. The goal is safe lending, not maximum lending.

A well-prepared loan proposal, transparent information, and realistic expectations significantly improve approval chances. Technology-driven tools and paperless processes can further make banking faster, safer, and more efficient for everyone.
